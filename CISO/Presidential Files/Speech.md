“Hey everyone,

For those of you who don’t know me, my name is Mario. I’ve been involved in this club for 3 years, and have served as treasurer and assistant treasurer for two of those years.

During that time, I’ve seen how this club operates—what works, what doesn’t, and where we’re falling short.

And honestly, I think our club has a lot of untapped potential.

Right now, we have a space, we have equipment, and we have talented people—but we’re not fully using any of it.

The lab doesn’t feel like a lab anymore.

And that’s why I’m running.

Because I want to bring this club back to what it *can* be:  
a place where people build, experiment, and gain real-world experience.

---

If I’m elected, my focus comes down to three things.

---

### First: turning the lab back into a real research space.

We already have the tools—we just aren’t using them.

If you didn't know, right now we have about 10 - 15 Raspberry Pis, networking equipment like micro computers and WiFi pineapples, and other resources sitting in cabinets like soldering kits and this super cool crab robot kit we can build that connects to a raspberry pi, all unused—sometimes because we don’t even know what we have,, or we’re missing simple things like keyboards, and power cables for the monitors.

My first step would be to fix that.

Over the summer, I want to inventory everything, replace essential equipment, and make the lab actually usable again.

But more importantly, I want to give the lab purpose.

That means turning the club room into a space for hands-on projects—like using the raspberry PI's to build small cyber environments for students to practice things like blue team defense, red team attacks, and system administration in a controlled setting.

The ideas with raspberry pi's are limitless, we could create a SOC environment, practice using self hosted services, or connect them to other devices like microcontrollers to research IoT to name a few.

I am also open to investing in newer technology like Raspberry Pi 5's, LoRa - WAN devices, and Mobile Phones for researching technologies like GrapheneOS

This would create opportunities for students to practice for competitions, do research, and provide opportunities for students who can't attend the Friday projects.

---

### Second: making the club more fair, transparent, and structured.

I want this club to run in a way that’s consistent, professional, and accountable.

That starts with the basics.

Things like keeping our website updated, keeping our officer board in the back updated, and making sure the lab space itself is maintained and usable.

These might seem like small things, but they set the tone.

I also want to set clear expectations for how the club communicates and operates.

That means no more closing the lab without explanation, no last-minute cancellations without reason, and making sure that the treasure has set mandatory hours where they need to make themselves available to collect dues.

I want to encourage collaboration between CISO cabinet and competition coaches to give them our support and ensure that the selection process is fair and transparent.

One thing I want to introduce to the CISO Club is monthly town halls—on the first Tuesday of every month. It'd be a time where we can talk openly about club decisions, goals, challenges, and opportunities to make decisions on what technology club money should be spent on for research.

Because if we’re collecting dues, that money should go back to the members.

And just as important, I want to establish clear behavioral standards in the lab.

This should be a professional and respectful space for everyone.

That includes zero tolerance for harassment, racism, or sexism—and I’m willing to back that up by introducing and enforcing bylaws to support those standards.

Because at the end of the day, structure and accountability are what allow everything else in this club to actually work.

---

### Third: Expand the club's reach and ambition

Right now, a lot of the technical communities on campus are separated—we’re in one room, robotics is in another, computer engineering is somewhere else.

I want to start bringing those groups together.

That starts with strengthening our relationship with WiCyS, our sister club.  
I want to develop and maintain communication between our leaders, shared visibility in our space, and offer more support for their projects and club growth.

Beyond that, I want to create real collaboration opportunities with other clubs—like robotics, computer science, and computer engineering.

Not just networking—but actual shared projects.

For example, allowing computer engineering majors to tinker with our broken clubroom equipment, or building that robot I mentioned earlier with the robotics club, installing a Pi on it, and inviting the computer science to write their own custom software on it. 

I also want us to look beyond just our campus.

Right now, we’re mostly operating in our own bubble.

I want to change that by building connections with other universities, inviting club members to join me in attending events, and creating more opportunities for members to network and learn from the broader Southern California community.

Because this club shouldn’t just be part of campus—it should be part of a larger cybersecurity community.

---

At the end of the day, this isn’t about adding more ideas.

It’s about giving this club direction.

If you want a club that’s more hands-on, more transparent, and more ambitious—

then I’d really appreciate your support.

Thank you.”
